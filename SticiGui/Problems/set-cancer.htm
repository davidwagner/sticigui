 To compare breast cancer risk across towns the investigators used a statistic known as the standardized incidence ratio (SIR), a commonly used tool for comparing disease rates. Incidence is the number of newly diagnosed cases in a given location (here, town) during a given time period (here, 1982-1994). The term "ratio" appears in SIR because it is necessary to account for different town population sizes. For example, suppose Town A has 10 new cases of cancer, while Town B has 5 new cases of cancer in a certain time period. Which town has a bigger cancer problem? It's hard to tell without knowing the town population sizes. If Town A has 1,000 people and Town B has 50 people, then 1 in 100 people in Town A were diagnosed with cancer, while  the rate was 10 in 100 people in Town B. Therfore, based on just these numbers, or statistics, Town B has a bigger cancer probnlem even though they have fewer (5 < 10) cancer cases. Although the SIR is not calculated in the same way, this example illustrates the idea of accounting for population size when comparing disease cases across different locations.

A risk factor for a disease is something that has been found to be associated with the disease, but necessarily causing the disease. In trying to explain why the breast cancer rates are higher on Cape Cod than non-Cape Massachusettes researchers frequently ask whether known risk factors could be responsible for the obsereved rate differences. For excample, age is a known risk factor: breast cancer is more common in older women than in younger women. Therefore, since Cape Cod is home to many retirees, this may explain the rate difference and not anything unique or intrinsic to Cape Cod, such as the air in the Cape. Trying to determine if the rate differences could be explained by known risk factors is a statistical problem to which their are both simple and elaborate approaches.
