Chapter 0

Why Statistics?

Most mathematics courses teach students how to solve very specific problems. Two examples are solving quadratic equations and finding unknown measures of angles in a geometric diagram. Few, however, teach students how to reason mathematically, perhaps combining different techniques to solve one problem. Statistics is often taught as a mathematics subject with seemingly independent parts (chapters). This chapter introduces basic ideas and concepts of statistical thinking and reasoning which are necessary to an understanding of the mathematical side of subject. A major theme of the chapter is on the consequences of being completely ignorant of chance and statistical ideas.
 

 

Quantitative Literacy

For many years now our schools and local, state, and federal governments have been concerned with illiteracy among students and adults. As a result many reading programs have been established to make sure that the citizens of the United States have basic skills in reading. Clearly, the consequences of an illiterate population would be detrimental. What has not been so well accepted is that having a citizenship ill-prepared  in quantitative reasoning and skills can be just as detrimental, not only to their personal well-being, but also to society.

Over the past 15 years or so there has been a lot of discussion on the importance of "quantitative literacy,"  partly because changes in our society increasingly require quantitative skills. More than ever, our society depends on computers, using them to store, retrieve, and analyze vast amounts of data. In a recent collection of articles on quantitative literacy (Steen, 1997) all the authors argue - from various perspectives - that individuals lacking quantitative skills will be at a significant disadvantage. Lynn Arthur Steen, the editor of the articles, says:

Ever since computers opened the floodgates of the information age, we have been caught in a rising tide of numbers. The ascendency of quantitative information has changed profoundly not only the environment in which we live and work, but also the entire framework of civic life...[The] constructs of modern civilation depend at their deepest level on quantitative information. Does the Dow average really measure the state of the economy? How can we best evaluate the quality of health care? Do polls influence or just predict how people will vote? How strong is the evidence linking estrogen supplemenst to increased rates of breast cancer in older women? Do the new bar code readers make more or fewer mistakes than trained checkout clerks? How secure are bank accounts from ATM fraud?

Numeracy is the new literacy of our age. As the printing press gave the power of letters to the masses, so the computer gives the power of numbers to ordinary citizens...But just as literacy was relatively rare five centuries ago, so is numeracy today. Only one in ten U.S. adults can reliably solve problems that require two or more steps. Even fewer can comprehend the complexity of issues that underlie clinical trials of new treatments for AIDS or think through the implications of a flat-tax system...The majority of today's high school graduates - not to mention dropouts - still lack fundamental "walking around" skills in quantitative literacy.

In the same book, Iddo Gal,  former director of the Adult Numeracy Project at the National Center on Adult Literacy, notes that today "adults face quantitative tasks in multiple situations whose contexts require seemless integration of numeracy and literacy skills." Some of these situations include home (e.g., coordinating schedules); personal finance (e.g., monitoring expenses and filling out tax forms); social action (e.g., design a survey for a local action group); work place (e.g., statistical process control and optimizing resources); active parenting (e.g., understanding standardized tests and statistics about schools). Some of these situations may only require interpretation:
 

These demand that people make sense of, and grasp the implications of, verbal or text-based messages that may rest on quntitaive issues but that do not involve direct manipulation of numbers; for example, when reading a newspaper or watching news on TV, being faced with results of a recent poll or medical experiment, possibly involving percentages, averages, rates of change, etc. Such situations may sometimes involve no numbers at all but still refer to important ideas that are part of mathematics or statistics, as when samples, bias, correlation, or causality are implied.
 

The main message of these and other authors is that today more and more situations require quantitative skills for proper solution or management. Since most of these situations involve data that are used to draw conclusions, individuals need to understand:
 

    • how their particular data arise or are collected
    • how to summarize the data
    • how to interpret results based on their analyses of the data

Although mathematics is important to these skills, it is perhaps more important to learn the underlying statistical ideas and methods. Some examples of the consequences of a lack of quantitative literacy ("innumeracy") are given below. The main goal of this book is teach you a part of statistics you need in everyday life.
 

Useful Stuff for the Real World

As we went through our usual mathematics courses in primary school, many of us wondered whether any of the mathematics we were learning would ever be useful. It is very easy to point to a lot of mathematics that is not directly related to everyday life, but it is also easy to point to mathematical tools that are extremely useful. Some of the most useful are statistical.
 

Strength in Numbers

St. John's Wort (Hypericum perforatum) is an herbal extract that is receiving a lot of attention as a mood enhancer, especially in improving depression, anxiety, and insomia. The following excerpt from a Newsweek article (5 May 1997) is typical of the many success stories of St. John's Wort.
 

                  Karin Taylor's black moods were often accompanied by inexplicable bouts of
                  insomnia, crying and lethargy. By last summer she had sunk so low she didn't care
                  if she lived or died. But Taylor balked when her physician suggested a common
                  antidepressant: she didn't feel comfortable taking drugs. Fortunately, she says, a
                  friend visiting from California suggested a natural herb called Saint John's Wort.
                  Within three weeks, Taylor's depression had lifted. "I feel restored," says the
                  58-year-old Toronto accountant, who continues to take two herb capsules daily.
                  "I'm my normal self again".
 

Like Karin Taylor, thousands of people report that St. John's Wort (SJW) has significantly improved their depression and are recommending the herbal extract to their relatives and friends. SJW is not an herb that has recently been discovered. It has been used for about 2,000 years by different cultures to ward off 'evil spirits.' Its use in the United States is relatively new. Because SJW has been used for a few thousand years to ward off evil spirits and now thousands of people swear by its positive effects on depression, it would seem that SJW is indeed a useful remedy for various psychological symptoms. The strength of the evidence here is that so many people are convinced of its efficacy.

Another example where the main argument depended on citing a large number of positive experiences is taken from an article (Steen, 1997) written by Gina Kolata, a science writer for the New York Times. She reports on the opposition by many parents in Princeton, New Jersey to classes that combine students from two grades. In support of the nongraded program, someone informed the school board that: "In a review of 64 studies, cited in ASCD's Educational Leadership issue of October 1992, research clearly supports the use of nongraded programs."

The strength of the argument in both of the above examples is on the number of positive reports. What was not explicitly addressed was the quality and reliability of the data. By this we mean specifying:

  1. where the data came from (e.g., a study;  informal survey; hearsay)
  2. who collected the data (e.g., radio station; research scientists in academia; private corporation)
  3. the subjects (e.g., a random sample of people; friends; white females age 18-30)
     

This list does not completely define quality and reliability of data. It does, however, provide some guidelines for assessing validity of evidence. For example, in the St. John's Wort case nothing was said about "clinical studies" comparing SJW to established antidepressants or whether the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) recognizes SJW as a treatment for depression. These are two important questions that we should ask of all "remedies" that are reported to be effective against some illness. All the evidence given in this example is anecdotal. One of the main reasons that andecdotal evidence is not reliable in this case is because people are more apt to report positive experiences. No doubt there are many people who have tried SJW without any mood enhancement. More than likely we don't hear about it because they have forgotten about trying SJW since it had no effect. In general, no matter how many people report positive results, anecdotal evidence is not on equal par with a well-designed clinical trial (chapter x).

The example on nongraded programs did not say anything about the studies other than that they favored combined classes. Without knowing more about the studies we should not conclude that combined grade levels are better than separate grade levels.
 
 

Statistical Lesson 0.1

That a large number of reports (by individuals or studies) have the same opinion or conclusion does not mean that something has been reliably demonstrated.

The Life of a Statistic

There are many statistics on cancer. One type of cancer that is receiving a lot of attention these days is breast cancer, which is reported to be the second leading cause of death in women. For example, it is often reported that:

                            *    One in eight women will get breast cancer in their lifetimes.

                            *    Every 11 minutes another woman dies of breast cancer.

These statistics say or mean different things to different people. If they are misinterpreted or taken as gospel they could cause a lot of unnecessary anxiety. In a collection of articles on judgement under uncertainty (Kahneman, Slovic, and Tversky, 1982), Max Singer writes about the "vitality of mythical numbers." He reminds us that many numbers or statistics have no basis in fact and that "because an estimate has been used widely by a variaety of people who should know what they're talking about , one cannot assume that the estimate is even approximately correct." Does this mean that we should not trust any numbers reported by such people as scientists, journalists, and politicians? No. What it does mean, however, is that to take a statistic seriously we need to be willing to investigate the life of the statistic: who's responsible for it? how did it come about? does it make sense relative to general knowledge? what does it imply?

What then are we to make of  the breast cancer statistics? Let's first consider the second statistic: every 11 minutes another woman dies of breast cancer. Should we take this statistic literally? To answer this question we have to know how the number was determined. Link to the the Breast Cancer Fact Sheet sponsored by the Breast Cancer Information Service,  find the 11 minute statistic, and then return to this page. We are not told how the statistic was determined, but we are given its source, the National Breast Cancer Coalition. With the internet it would be very easy to find the web site for this organization and then try to track down the basis for the statistic. This exercise will be pursued below. For the moment let's think about how we might arrive at the statistic; there is a hint in the fact sheet. We are told that in 1991, 43,582 women died of breast cancer. Dividing 43,582 by the total number of minutes in a year (525,600) we find: 43,582 deaths per year / 525,600 minutes per year = 1 death per 12.06 minutes

We don't get 11, but our reasoning gets us close to what has been reported. The statistic, therefore, would appear to have some basis. An interpretation of the above result is that every 12 minutes a woman dies of breast cancer. However, as the calculation shows, the number of deaths have been evenly distributed throughout the year without knowledge of when the deaths actually occurred. Therefore, the statement that "every 12 minutes a woman dies of breast cancer" is an approximation to what actually happened. For example, it might have happened that no such deaths occurred in the second week of October.

Let us now consider the 1 in 8 statistic. Again, the fact sheet gives the National Breast Cancer Coalition as the source of the number. What does the number mean? It does not mean that at this moment, any given woman has a 1 in 8 chance of developing or "getting" breast cancer. The fact is that the incidence ( number of new cases in a population within a given time period) of breast cancer rises with age. The National Cancer Institute gives the following incidence rates:
 
 
 

Breast cancer incidence rate
(per 100,000)
Age group Rate
20-24 .9
30-34 26.6
40-44 128.3

 
 The 1 in 8 statistic is a lifetime incidence rate: among women who live to be 95 years old, 1 in 8 of them will get breast cancer sometime in their lifetime. This information puts the 1 in 8 statistic in a different perspective. And the same will be true for other statistics that are frequently reported and cited if they are properly investigated. It may sound like a lot of work just to understand a number, but we hope that we have convinced you of the importance of taking numbers seriously. When gone unchecked, statistics can take on a life of their own. Did you hear the one about 1 in 4 college women experiencing date rape?
 
 
 

Statistical Lesson 0.2

It is imporatnt to know the basis of a statistic; even if it is one that is highly publicized by reputable people, groups, or organizations. Their intended meaning might not be what is being reported or interpreted.

 
 

Numbers Set in Stone

Stephen Jay Gould is a well known scientist who teaches biology, geology, and the history of science at Harvard. He has written several popular book on science. The following is an excerpt from an article (Discover, June 1995) he wrote that discusses a popular statistic that attempts to measure the "middle" of a list of numbers or a data set.
 
 
 

In 1982, I learned I was suffering from a rare and serious cancer. After surgery, I asked my doctor what the best technical literature on the cancer was. She told me, with a touch of diplomacy, that there was nothing really worth reading. I soon realized why she had offered that humane advice: my cancer is incurable, with a median mortality of eight months after discovery.
 
 

The statistic of interest in this case is "median mortality of eight months." Again, we have a number -- 8 months -- that has been provided by a reputable person, namely, the surgeon. Now, the surgeon probably knows what the statistic means, but many of her patients will not know. Even if people do not know what median mortality means they may have some preconceived notion of "similar" statistics or they may form a "definition" on the spot. There is also the business of median mortality after discovery. What does this mean? Does "after discovery" matter? As before, we begin by stating what is not meant by "median mortality of eight months after discovery."  The eight month figure is not a limit: the statistic does not mean that at the eighth month after being diagnosed with the cancer you will die. Clearly, Professor Gould is a counterexample to this nonsense. (Why?) To find out what it actually means read the article by Gould.

The point of this example is that many numbers (in "real life") are reported or interpreted as if they are followed by everyone. To wit, everyone who has the same cancer afflicting Professor Gould will die after 8 months of diagnosis. The fact of the matter is that some will survive much longer than 8 months, while some will live only a few months after diagnosis. In statistical jargon we say that people vary according to their survival times after diagnosis. That's the way it is with most statistics that are reported. More obvious examples abound. The average salary of a new electrical enginner may be $48,000 per year, but not all such engineers make this salary.
 
 
 
 

Statistical Lesson 0.3

There is variation underlying most statistics. Not everyone is the same.

Gambler's Fallacy

A gambler playing a game of dice in Las Vegas has lost seven times in a row, losing $50 each time. Should she continue to play? Some may argue that she's having bad luck and should stop. Others may argue that she should continue to play, maybe increasing her bet, because by the "law of averages" she is bound to win after losing so many times. The latter is a myth and is known as the gambler's fallacy: in playing the same game over and over, the chance of winning is not more likely after a string of losses than it was at the first game. Similarly, the chance of losing is not more likely after a string of wins than it was at the first game. This fallacy, or some version of it, is invoked in many other situations; for example:

a.
A telemarketer tries you at home everyday for two weeks between 7 and 9pm, but does not get an answer (assume you do not have an answering machine). The supervisor asks how long it will be before the telemarketer thinks you will be reached during those times. The telemarketer invokes the gambler's fallacy and says that it shouldn't be long, now.
b.
A baseball announcer observes that a certain player has not hit in his last 22 at bats. "By the law of avergaes," he says, "he's due to hit soon, and will probably go into a hitting streak."
c.
Many state lottos are based on matching numbers. For example, to win in the Texas State Lotto you must match the six numbers drawn from among fifty (numbered 1,2, 3, ... , 50). If the number 30 comes up in eight consecutive games it is highly unlikely to show up in the next game.
d.
John has been turned down for a date by the last 6 women he has asked out. Not surprisingly he is depressed. His friends have encourgaed him to keep trying. Afterall, they say, the law of averages promises a date in the near future.

The law of averages applies only under certain sircumstances, and should not be blindly invoked. If misapplied it can lead to wrong conclusions. We will discuss the law of averages in more detail in later chapters. For now the most important things to remember are:

1. The law of averages does not work by compensation. In example (c), the chance that the number 30 does not show up in the ninth game is the same as it is in any other game, namely 88%.

2. The law of averages only applies to events that are repeated under identical conditions. The Texas State Lotto in example c is played under identical conditions each Wednesday and Saturday. In example d, the situations under which John requests a date are not identical because each woman is different.

Do the Results Apply to Me?

For over 50 years psychologists have relied on the Minnesota Multiphasic Psychological Inventory (MMPI) to help evaluate and diagnose mental disorders. The first version of the MMPI was developed in 19xx by investigators at the University of Minnesota Hospital. The subjects used to develop the test were visitors and patients of the hospital's psychiatric ward. In 1989 a second version of the MMPI, the MMPI-2, was developed. The MMPI-2 used a larger sample of people from across the country than that used to develop the original MMPI.

How reliable a test was the original MMPI? If you were a 55 year old Mexican-American woman in 1975 and a psychologist used the MMPI to help evaluate you for depression, how much faith would you have in it? The patients used to develop the original MMPI were a special group of people. They were from the Midwest and associated with the university's psychiatric ward in the 19xx's. It is unlikely that Mexican-American women were represented in the subject group. Is there reason to believe that the items which detected depression in the Minnesota subjects should also apply to the Mexican-American woman? Clearly, if there is no basis for extrapolation to individuals like yourself, there is no reason to believe that studies based on subjects different from yourself should apply to you.

From a statistical perspective the original MMPI should not be used on subjects which do not match pertinent characteristics of the Minnesota subjects. The problem with the original MMPI is its design. It is interesting to note that in a 1993 survey of clinicians about 25% said that they would continue to use the original MMPI. Knowledge of some basic statistical ideas and principles are very important to the design of "instruments" such as the MMPI, surveys, polls, and experiments.
 

A more extreme case is provided by growth charts showing how a child's height and weight change with age. In the weight chart there is a white band showing where the weights are of most boys of a given age. For example, approximately 95% of the ten year old boys (represented in this chart) weigh 100 pounds or less, 50% weigh 70 pounds or less, and 5% weigh 55 pounds or less. The weights 100, 70, and 55 are the 95th, 50th, and 5th percentiles of the weights of 10 year old boys. These percentiles can be read off the chart for other ages in a similar manner. The first growth charts were based on 100 white boys and 100 white girls from Wisconsin. (**check on the state**) Therefore, if other races do not grow at the same rate as whites, then the first growth charts were not appropriate for evaluating the growth of non-whites. In the above growth charts there is no mention of race or the number of boys that are represented in the chart. It would be helpful to know at least this much more to make the charts more trustworthy and useful.

If the data do not represent your particular characteristics, the conclusions may not apply to you.

Assessment of Quantitative Literacy

As mentioned at the beginning of this chapter, quantitative literacy has received a lot of attention relatively recently. In particular, the U.S. government has and continues to sponsor quantitative literacy programs,as well assess quantitative skills of the U.S. population. A leader in the field of quantitative skills assessment is the National Center for Educational Statistics. Their purpose is to collect and report "...statistics and information showing the condition and progress of education in the United States and other nations in order to promote and accelerate the improvement of American education." One way that they accomplish their mission is by conducting surveys. Two surveys that deal explicitly with educational assessment are the National Assessment of Education Progress (NAEP) and the  National Adult Literacy Survey (NALS):

            National Assessment of Education Progress: This survey is designed to continuously monitor the
             knowledge, skills, and performance of the nation's children and youth. NAEP provides objective data on
             student performance at national and regional levels in reading, mathematics, science, writing, citizenship,
             U.S. history, geography, socials studies, art, music, literature, computer competence, and career and occupational
             development.

             National Adult Literacy Survey: The 1992 National Adult Literacy Survey assessed the literacy skills of
             the U.S. adult population (ages 16 and over living in households), using simulations of three kinds of
             literacy tasks that adults would ordinarily encounter in daily life (prose literacy, document literacy, and
             quantitative literacy). Representative data are available for 12 states (CA, FL, IL, IN, IA, LA, NJ, NY, OH, PA,
             TX, WA) and for adults in prison. Participants also answered questions about their demographic characteristics,
             educational backgrounds, reading practices, and labor market experiences.
 

The data that are collected from these and other surveys and other organizations must be analyzed, summarized, and reported in a meaningful way. Consider these questions posed by the National Center for Education Statistics (NCES):
 

Is student enrollment growing or shrinking? What is the trend in teachers' salaries? How do states
compare in teacher/student ratios and educational attainment? Is the cost of college continuing to
rise?

 
To address these questions the NCES relies on statistical methods:
 

Finding the answers to these and many other questions is more than just a trivial pursuit. Statistics
are the directional signs along the road to quality education for all Americans. We must know
where we have been, so that we can better understand where we are going.

Most Americans have memories of their own formal education and have strong opinions based on
those experiences. Personal contact is a powerful learning tool, but no single experience, no
matter how powerful, can compare with facts gathered from a broader perspective. Such is the
wealth of statistical material...

 
We will return to the NAEP and NALS surveys in later chapters to answer some specific questions regarding the state of education in the United States. For now it is enough for you to know that without statistics it would not be possible to make sense out of the enormous amount of data collected on education. Education is a very important part of all our lives. It is not an abstarct topic discussed in the classroom; it is as "real" as it gets. This is another case where statistics proves useful in the "real" world.

Hopefully by now you have been convinced that it is worthwhile learning a little about statistics. If not, simply do some research of your own by trying to find areas where statistics does not apply. You would be hard-pressed to find more than a handful! There are many definitions of quantitative literacy, but most include some aspects of statistical reasoning and basic technique. When you are done working through this book you will have learned some basic ideas, principles, and techniques of statistics. We're sure that you will find them surprisingly useful in other courses and life beyond the classroom (real life?)
